Introduction

The motherboard is a circuit board that essentially connects all components of a computer together and allows those components to communicate with each other. The design, size, and features of a motherboard significantly impact the capabilities, expansion options, and compatibility of a system. The development of the motherboard is very unique and has an impact on the market compared to other hardware devices because of how its development would affect these other devices depending on their compatibility with each other. There are different types of motherboards that have various uses such as standard desktop use, high-end gaming or industrial systems. This study will cover the key differences among several types of motherboards based on factors such as form factor, build, CPU and memory slots, chipset compatibility, BIOS features, PCI slots, SATA ports, and built-in features.

Discussion

Motherboards can be categorized into several types, mainly based on form factors which define its physical dimensions and layout. Form factors also impact other parts like the number of components that can be connected, expansion capabilities, cooling requirements, and power consumption. Each type of motherboard is specialized for a specific set of use cases.

  1. AT Motherboard (Advanced Technology)

    2. One of the earliest types of motherboards, developed and popularized in the late 20th century. It is characterized by its large size which limits its use in compact cases. It typically supports older processors and expansion slots, making it mostly irrelevant today.

  2. ATX Motherboard (Advanced Technology eXtended)

    3. The most common form factor today, offering a balance between size and expansion capability. It is used in a wide range of systems from basic desktop setups to high-performance gaming PCs.

  3. BTX Motherboard (Balanced Technology eXtended)

    4. Developed as an improvement over ATX, the BTX motherboard aimed to provide better airflow and cooling but did not gain widespread popularity compared to ATX. It has been largely phased out in favor of the ATX motherboard.

  4. Extended-ATX Motherboard

    5. A larger version of the ATX, the Extended-ATX motherboard provides more space for components and supports additional PCI slots and RAM slots, making it ideal for workstations and servers.

  5. LPX Motherboard (Low Profile eXtended)

    6. An older form factor designed for low-profile systems, the LPX motherboard was known for its compact design. It featured a riser card for expansion slots but had limited upgrade options, leading to their replacement of newer standard motherboards.

  6. Micro-ATX Motherboard

    7. A smaller variant of ATX, the Micro-ATX board offers fewer expansion slots but maintains compatibility with most components. They are popular in budget builds and smaller desktop systems.

  7. Mini ITX Motherboard (Mini-Information Technology eXtended)

    8. An even smaller form factor known for its compact size and low power consumption. They are ideal for small form factor builds like home theater PCs.

  8. Mini-ATX Motherboard

    9. A rare form factor that falls between Micro-ATX and Mini ITX in size, providing a compact and expandable design.

  9. Pico BTX Motherboard

    10. A compact version of the BTX standard, designed for small and efficient systems. It offers limited expandability but focuses more on cooling and low power consumption.

  10. Standard-ATX Motherboard

    11. A common term often used interchangeably with the ATX standard. It balances size with expandability and is a common choice for many desktop builds.
